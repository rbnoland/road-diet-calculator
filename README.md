# About This Tool

## What is a road diet?

A road diet is a way of redesigning a street to make it safer without closing it or significantly reducing its capacity. The most common type — and the one this tool focuses on — converts a four-lane road into a three-lane road: one lane in each direction, plus a shared center turn lane.

The center turn lane removes a major source of crashes. On a traditional four-lane road, drivers turning left must slow or stop across a live lane of traffic, creating rear-end and side-swipe collisions. The center turn lane gives turning drivers a dedicated space to wait, out of the flow of through traffic. Research by the Federal Highway Administration (FHWA) consistently finds that road diets reduce crashes by roughly 19–47%, with a typical reduction around 29%.

## What does this calculator do?

This tool helps transportation planners, engineers, and municipal staff answer a simple but important question:

> **Is a road diet worth the investment?**

It does this by comparing two things in dollar terms:

- **What you gain** — the value of crashes that are expected to be prevented
- **What you give up** — the cost of restriping the road, plus any extra travel time caused by a lower speed limit

When the value of prevented crashes exceeds the costs, the project has a positive return on investment.

## What can users calculate?

### The value of preventing crashes

Users enter the number of crashes that currently occur on the road each year, broken down by how serious they are. The tool converts those crash counts into a total annual dollar value using federal cost figures — from a minor fender-bender at a few thousand dollars up to a fatal crash, which the U.S. Department of Transportation values at $13.7 million based on what society is willing to pay to reduce the risk of death. These figures are then projected forward over the full life of the project and converted to today's dollars.

### The cost of the project

Users enter the cost to restripe the road. They also enter the road's current and intended speed limits. If the speed limit goes down, drivers take slightly longer to travel the corridor — the tool calculates the value of that added time for both regular drivers and commercial trucks, and adds it to the total cost.

### Whether the investment pays off

The tool combines these figures to produce three key results:

- **Net Present Value (NPV)** — the total financial benefit of the project in today's dollars, after subtracting all costs. A positive number means the project returns more value than it costs.
- **Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR)** — how many dollars of benefit are produced for every dollar spent. A ratio above 1.0 means the project pays for itself; a ratio of 3.0 means it returns $3 for every $1 invested.
- **Break-even threshold** — the minimum crash reduction needed for the project to break even. If FHWA research suggests a 29% reduction is typical and the break-even point is 5%, the project has a large margin of safety.

### A range of scenarios

Because no one can predict exactly how much a road diet will reduce crashes on any given street, the tool includes a slider. Users can move it from 0% to 100% crash reduction and see instantly how the NPV and BCR change. This helps planners communicate the range of likely outcomes to decision-makers and the public.

### A year-by-year breakdown

The tool also shows how costs and benefits accumulate over time — which year the project breaks even, and what the total return looks like by the end of the analysis period.

## Who is this tool for?

This calculator is designed for **transportation planners, traffic engineers, and local government staff** who need to document the economic case for a road safety project — for grant applications, council presentations, or internal project evaluation. It follows standard U.S. Department of Transportation guidance for benefit-cost analysis, so its outputs are suitable for use in federal funding applications.

No specialist software or technical background is required. The tool runs entirely in a web browser and produces a printable summary report that can be attached to any project submission.

**Community members and safety advocates** can also use this tool to build an evidence-based case for change on a street they know. If you live near a dangerous road or have been pushing your local council to act, this calculator gives you something concrete to bring to that conversation. By entering publicly available crash data — which most state and local transportation agencies publish online — alongside basic road information, anyone can produce the same kind of benefit-cost analysis that planners use internally. The printable summary is designed to be legible to a non-technical audience, making it a practical handout for community meetings, public comment submissions, or conversations with elected officials. Showing that a safety improvement pays for itself many times over, in the government's own accounting framework, is often one of the most persuasive arguments available to advocates.
